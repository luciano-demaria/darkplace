I wake up on the red carpeted floor of a library. There are some shelves behind a circulation desk ahead
of me and an open door off to the right. A warm breeze drifts in from behind me, where I notice a cracked
entry door. I can't quite see from here, but looks bright outside.





I'm near the entrance of the library. There are some shelves behind the circulation desk ahead
of me and an open door off to the right. The entry door is still cracked open with a warm breeze
accompanied by streams of flickering light. It smells smokey.




As I walk up to the circulation desk, I notice a distinct lack of computers or checkout systems. Instead,
a journal sits on the desk alongside a fountain pen. There's also a CD player and a pair of headphones towards
one end of the desk.


The journal rests on the desk alongside a fountain pen. The CD player waits patiently.


On the first page of the journal, a page header is written in a meticulous, almost machine-like
calligraphic style:

"What is Real?"

The rest of the page is blank.



The second page of the journal is completely blank. In fact, the rest of the journal appears to be
be blank. There's a hastily-folded note tucked between the second and third pages.


Whoever started writing this essay probably won't mind if I add a few thoughts of my own. I treat the header like a prompt and write:



I begin to write:

(Here I will compare Lacan's concept of 'the Real' with Dimlose, the area in the game I feel is closest to reaching the impossibility of fully representing the Real.)

I begin to write:

(Here I will argue with myself about whether the Dark Place can be considered hyperreal in the sense that Beaudrilliard writes about.)

Upon moving closer, I realize that the wooden shelves are actually made of concrete. The aisles appear to be ordered alphabetically
with some having end caps that display a variety of books, magazines, and CDs.





The note reads like the beginning of an essay:

"Welcome to the Dark Place is a case study in the buildup of tension through impeding the player's
senses. With little to see other than a black screen and some text descriptions, deep listening is one of
the only methods by which the player is allowed to percieve the game world."


On the back of the note, more is written in tiny scrawls:

"Ernest H. Lindley writes: 'The animals most gifted with experimenting techniques, those which utilize changes
in environment most promptly, will be favored in the struggle for existence.'[ 1 ] The Dark Place is dreamlike, and
it does not function in the same way that our reality functions. It serves as an unexplored environment for
us to adapt to. Of course, adaptation often manifests as the result of failure-"

The handwriting becomes less and less legible until it becomes chicken scratch.


1. Lindley, Ernest H. “A Study of Puzzles with Special Reference to the Psychology of Mental Adaptation.” The American Journal of Psychology 8, no. 4 (1897): 431–93. https://doi.org/10.2307/1411772.
